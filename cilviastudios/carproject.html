CFD Car Project

Designing a car and simulating computational fluid dynamics to calculate the car's drag coefficent.

By: Mustafa Al Quraishi.

Problem: With the ever-growing current electric car market, electric cars are poised to become the next mainstream iteration of personal transport. Barriers to the current mass adoption of electric cars are fundamentally down to lack of charging infrastructure, long charging times and short ranges.

Solution: A concept electric urban small SUV hatchback has been proposed with aerodynamics in mind to reduce overall drag upon the car; thus, improving rate of charge use in turn increasing range.

Design Development

Results

The model was sculpted in Fusion 360 then computational fluid dynamic tests were run in Solidworks 2019. The tests consisted of cut plot testing of air velocity, surface pressure modelling, and flow trajectories around the car’s body. An air velocity of 31.29 ms^-1 was set for all models.

Pressure modelling shows higher levels of pressure on the front fenders of the wheels, this was anticipated with the flaring of the wheel fenders. However, the overall homogeneity of the surface pressure demonstrates a successful design with no key areas of high pressure, due to the overall smooth and unaggressive surface curvature.

To calculate the battery capacity for this car with a drag coefficient of 0.32, and frontal area of 2.4 m^2, in a scenario we set the environment for a motor rated at 350V, an ideal range of 200km for the car travelling at 31.29 m/s through air with a density of 1.225 kg/m^3, (70mph). With a ratio of drag to total resistance of 0.65. The total drag force at this speed = 0.32 ∗ 0.5 ∗ 1.225 ∗ 31.29! ∗ 2.4 = 0.46 kN Thus the power to overcome the drag force at this speed = 0.46 * 31.29 = 14.4 kW Dividing by the drag to total resistance ratio we receive the total power required to overcome drag and rolling resistance = 22.17kW. Dividing by the motor voltage a amperage of 65.34A is required. Assuming a 200km range at 31.29m/s, that would take 1.78 hours and thus a 116 Ah battery is required. Multiplying by a design and safety factor of 2, the battery capacity would have to be minimum 232 Ah.
